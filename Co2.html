The Royal College of Physicians estimates that in the UK alone, 40,000 deaths a year are attributable to outdoor air pollution. Last year, an emissions surcharge, known as the "T-charge", was introduced to tackle pollution in London, which frequently misses air quality targets. It charges drivers of diesel and petrol vehicles registered before 2006 Dh52 a day to drive in central London during weekday daytime in an attempt to tackle air pollution. According to the RAC, this will include cars, vans, minibuses, buses, coaches and HGVs, motorbikes, motorised caravans and horseboxes, private ambulances, motor hearses, dual purpose vehicles and other specialist vehicle types that do not meet the minimum Euro 4 emission standard, a classification based on the age of the car.



“Vehicle emission accounts for 75-80% of Dubai’s air pollution, while the rest is due to emissions from manufacturing industries and power plants,” said Abdullah Raffia, assistant director general of Dubai Municipality for Environment and Public Health Affairs. According to Raffia, the survey aims to measure pollutant levels in a car’s exhaust while the vehicle is travelling on the road. It will detect the five major air pollutants: hydrocarbon, carbon monoxide, nitrogen oxides, carbon dioxide and smoke emissions. The survey will use remote sensing technology that eliminates the need to physically connect the device to a car. The municipality is working with the Dubai Roads & Transport Authority (RTA) and Dubai Police to carry out the project. According to the current law, driving a vehicle with excessive emissions that pollute the environment carries a fine of AED300. During the period 1999 to 2006, some 1115 vehicles were reported.
